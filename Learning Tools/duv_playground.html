How to read it: the slit pitch sets the diffraction angle — finer pitch throws the ±1, ±2, ±3 orders out to steeper angles. The lens can only catch orders that fall inside its NA. With just the 0 order the wafer washes out to a flat blur; add the ±1 order and the slits start to form but diffraction fringes ripple in; capture ±2 and the wafer reproduces the two-slit mask; capture ±3 and the edges get razor-sharp. The DOE shape is what rescues those high orders: each illumination pole hits the reticle from a different angle and slides a copy of the whole order-comb sideways in the pupil. A dipole (2 poles) can rescue one order; a quad (4 poles) reaches further; a hex (6 poles) pulls even the ±3 order back inside the NA. Try the DOE rescue preset, then switch dipole → quad → hex on the same pitch/NA and watch the fringes collapse into clean slits. Physics here is exaggerated for intuition, not to scale.
